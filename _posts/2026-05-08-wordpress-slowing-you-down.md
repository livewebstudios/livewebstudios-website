## WordPress Is Great — Until It Isn't

Let me be clear: WordPress isn't broken. For certain use cases — e-commerce, large editorial sites, anything with complex backend needs — it's still the right tool. But for the majority of small business websites? A five-page brochure site with a contact form and a blog? WordPress is like using a dump truck to pick up groceries.

It works. It's just a lot.

## What "A Lot" Looks Like in Practice

The average WordPress site runs somewhere between 20 and 60 plugins. Each plugin adds weight, adds potential security holes, and adds something that needs to be updated. Miss an update, and you're vulnerable. Update without testing, and you might break something.

Then there's the database. Every page load on a WordPress site is a round trip to a database, pulling data, assembling the page, and serving it. On a well-optimized setup, that happens fast. On a site that's been untouched for two years? It's slower than it looks.

## What the Data Says

Google's Core Web Vitals are now a ranking signal. That means page speed directly affects where you show up in search results. A site that takes 4 seconds to load on mobile is competing at a serious disadvantage against a site that loads in under a second.

We've been running audits on client sites throughout this migration campaign. The before-and-after numbers on load time are not subtle.

## What We're Doing About It

For clients who don't have e-commerce or complex backend needs, we're migrating to a modern static stack — built with AI tools, deployed through Netlify, hosted on GitHub. The result is a site that loads faster, costs less to host, and has essentially zero maintenance overhead.
